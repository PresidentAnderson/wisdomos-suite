Module 5: Ethical Living

Explore the foundations of moral philosophy, develop your ethical framework, and learn to live with integrity, purpose, and virtue in complex modern situations.

Lesson 5.1: Virtue Ethics

The Nature of Virtue

Virtue ethics focuses on character rather than actions or consequences. It asks not "What should I do?" but "What kind of person should I be?"

Aristotelian Virtues

Aristotle identified virtue as the mean between extremes:

Courage

Deficiency: Cowardice

Virtue: Courage

Excess: Recklessness

Acting despite fear when it's the right thing to do

Temperance

Deficiency: Insensibility

Virtue: Temperance

Excess: Self-indulgence

Moderation in pleasure and desire

Generosity

Deficiency: Stinginess

Virtue: Generosity

Excess: Prodigality

Giving appropriately to others

Honesty

Deficiency: Self-deprecation

Virtue: Honesty

Excess: Boastfulness

Truthfulness about oneself and situations

The Four Cardinal Virtues

  • Prudence (Wisdom): Right reason applied to practice
  • Justice: Giving each their due
  • Temperance: Moderation and self-control
  • Courage: Firmness in difficulties

Developing Virtue

Virtues are developed through:

  1. Practice: Virtue comes from habituation
  2. Role Models: Learning from virtuous examples
  3. Reflection: Understanding why actions are virtuous
  4. Community: Virtue is developed in relationship
  5. Purpose: Connecting virtue to life goals

Modern Virtues

Contemporary virtue ethics adds:

  • Compassion: Empathetic concern for others' suffering
  • Integrity: Consistency between values and actions
  • Humility: Accurate self-assessment and openness
  • Gratitude: Appreciation for what we have
  • Resilience: Bouncing back from adversity

Virtue Self-Assessment

Which virtue do you most need to develop? What specific steps will you take?

Lesson 5.2: Moral Decision Making

Ethical Frameworks

Different approaches to determining right from wrong:

Deontological Ethics (Kant)

Focus: Duty and rules

Key Question: Is the action itself right?

Principles:

  • Act only on maxims that could be universal laws
  • Treat people as ends, never merely as means
  • Some acts are always wrong regardless of consequences

Example: Lying is wrong even to save a life

Consequentialism (Mill/Bentham)

Focus: Outcomes and results

Key Question: What produces the best consequences?

Principles:

  • Actions are right if they produce good outcomes
  • Greatest good for the greatest number
  • The ends can justify the means

Example: Lying is acceptable if it saves lives

Care Ethics (Gilligan/Noddings)

Focus: Relationships and care

Key Question: What maintains and nurtures relationships?

Principles:

  • Ethics rooted in relationships
  • Emphasizes empathy and compassion
  • Context and particularity matter

Example: Consider impact on all relationships involved

Ethical Decision-Making Process

  1. Recognize the Ethical Issue
    • Could this decision harm someone?
    • Is there a choice between good and bad alternatives?
    • Is this a legal issue, ethical issue, or both?
  2. Get the Facts
    • What are the relevant facts?
    • Who are the stakeholders?
    • What are the options for action?
  3. Evaluate Alternative Actions
    • Which option produces the most good?
    • Which respects rights and dignity?
    • Which treats people fairly?
    • Which contributes to virtue?
  4. Make a Decision and Test It
    • How would I feel if this was public?
    • Would I want this done to me?
    • Does this align with my values?
  5. Act and Reflect
    • Implement the decision courageously
    • Monitor outcomes
    • Learn from the experience

Common Ethical Dilemmas

  • Truth vs. Loyalty: Honesty or protecting someone
  • Individual vs. Community: Personal rights vs. common good
  • Short-term vs. Long-term: Immediate needs vs. future consequences
  • Justice vs. Mercy: Fair punishment vs. compassion

Lesson 5.3: Living with Purpose

Finding Your Purpose

Purpose gives life meaning and direction. It's the intersection of:

The Japanese Concept of Ikigai

Your reason for being exists where four elements overlap:

  • What you love (Passion)
  • What you're good at (Vocation)
  • What the world needs (Mission)
  • What you can be paid for (Profession)

Where these intersect is your ikigai - your life's purpose

Elements of a Purposeful Life

1. Meaning-Making

  • Creating coherent life narratives
  • Finding significance in experiences
  • Connecting past, present, and future
  • Transcending self-interest

2. Value Alignment

  • Living according to core values
  • Making choices that reflect beliefs
  • Integrity between inner and outer life
  • Authentic self-expression

3. Contribution

  • Making a positive difference
  • Leaving a meaningful legacy
  • Serving something greater than self
  • Creating value for others

4. Growth

  • Continuous learning and development
  • Embracing challenges
  • Expanding capabilities
  • Actualizing potential

Practical Steps to Purpose

  1. Explore Your Values
    • What matters most to you?
    • What would you stand up for?
    • What brings you alive?
  2. Identify Your Strengths
    • What comes naturally to you?
    • What do others ask your help with?
    • When do you feel most capable?
  3. Recognize Needs
    • What problems do you see?
    • What suffering moves you?
    • What changes would you make?
  4. Create Your Mission
    • Write a personal mission statement
    • Set meaningful goals
    • Take purposeful action

Living Your Purpose Daily

  • Morning: Set intentions aligned with purpose
  • Decisions: Use purpose as a filter
  • Actions: Take small steps toward your mission
  • Evening: Reflect on purposeful moments
  • Weekly: Assess alignment and adjust

Module Exercises

Exercise 1: Virtue Practice Week

Choose one virtue to practice intensively for a week:

  1. Select a virtue you want to develop
  2. Define what it looks like in action
  3. Practice it in small ways daily
  4. Journal about challenges and successes
  5. Notice how it affects your relationships

Exercise 2: Ethical Dilemma Analysis

Think of an ethical dilemma you face:

  1. Describe the situation clearly
  2. Apply deontological ethics - what's your duty?
  3. Apply consequentialism - what produces best outcomes?
  4. Apply virtue ethics - what would a virtuous person do?
  5. Apply care ethics - what preserves relationships?
  6. Synthesize insights into a decision

Exercise 3: Purpose Mapping

Create your ikigai map:

  • List 10 things you love doing
  • List 10 things you're good at
  • List 10 needs you see in the world
  • List 10 things people would pay for
  • Find overlaps and patterns
  • Draft a purpose statement
