Module 3: Practical Philosophy

Philosophy becomes wisdom when applied to daily life. This module explores timeless philosophical practices that can transform your everyday experience.

Lesson 3.1: Stoic Practices

The Stoic Foundations

Stoicism teaches us to focus on what we can control and accept what we cannot. Core principles include:

The Dichotomy of Control

Within Our Control

  • Our judgments and interpretations
  • Our decisions and choices
  • Our attitudes and responses
  • Our values and priorities
  • Our efforts and actions

Outside Our Control

  • Others' actions and opinions
  • Past and future events
  • Our reputation
  • Our body and health (partially)
  • External circumstances

Daily Stoic Practices

1. Morning Reflection (Marcus Aurelius)

Begin each day by reminding yourself:

"Today I will meet with interference, ingratitude, insolence, disloyalty, ill-will, and selfishness. But I will not be surprised or disturbed, for the world is full of such people."

2. Evening Review (Seneca)

Before bed, ask yourself:

  • What did I do well today?
  • What could I have done better?
  • What did I learn?
  • How can I improve tomorrow?

3. Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum)

Occasionally imagine losing what you value to:

  • Cultivate gratitude
  • Reduce attachment
  • Prepare mentally for setbacks
  • Appreciate the present moment

4. View from Above

Imagine viewing your situation from a cosmic perspective to gain clarity and reduce anxiety about minor concerns.

Lesson 3.2: Buddhist Mindfulness

The Four Foundations of Mindfulness

1. Mindfulness of Body (Kayanupassana)

  • Breath awareness
  • Body scanning
  • Awareness of postures
  • Walking meditation

2. Mindfulness of Feelings (Vedananupassana)

  • Pleasant sensations
  • Unpleasant sensations
  • Neutral sensations
  • Not identifying with feelings

3. Mindfulness of Mind (Cittanupassana)

  • States of mind (calm, agitated, focused, scattered)
  • Observing thoughts without attachment
  • Recognizing mental patterns

4. Mindfulness of Mental Objects (Dhammanupassana)

  • The Five Hindrances (desire, aversion, sloth, restlessness, doubt)
  • The Seven Factors of Enlightenment
  • The Four Noble Truths

Practical Mindfulness Techniques

STOP Technique

  • S - Stop what you're doing
  • T - Take a breath
  • O - Observe your experience
  • P - Proceed with awareness

RAIN Method for Difficult Emotions

  • R - Recognize what's happening
  • A - Allow the experience to be there
  • I - Investigate with kindness
  • N - Non-identification; you are not your emotions

Loving-Kindness Meditation (Metta)

Systematically send good wishes:

  1. To yourself: "May I be happy, peaceful, and free from suffering"
  2. To loved ones
  3. To neutral people
  4. To difficult people
  5. To all beings everywhere

Lesson 3.3: Decision-Making Frameworks

Philosophical Decision Tools

1. The Categorical Imperative (Kant)

Before acting, ask:

  • Could this action become a universal law?
  • Am I treating people as ends in themselves, not merely as means?
  • Would I want to live in a world where everyone did this?

2. Utilitarian Calculus (Mill/Bentham)

Consider the consequences:

  • Who will be affected?
  • What are the potential benefits and harms?
  • What action produces the greatest good for the greatest number?

3. Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)

Ask yourself:

  • What would a virtuous person do?
  • Does this action develop good character?
  • Am I acting from virtue or vice?

Practical Decision Framework

The 10-10-10 Rule

How will I feel about this decision in:

  • 10 minutes?
  • 10 months?
  • 10 years?

The Regret Minimization Framework

Project yourself to age 80 and ask:

  • Will I regret not trying this?
  • Will I regret doing this?
  • What would I tell my younger self?

The Decision Journal

For important decisions, record:

  1. The situation and context
  2. The options considered
  3. Your reasoning and values involved
  4. The decision made
  5. Expected outcomes
  6. Actual outcomes (review later)
  7. Lessons learned

Module Exercises

Exercise 1: One Week of Stoic Practice

Choose one Stoic practice to implement for a week:

  • Morning reflection on potential challenges
  • Evening review of the day
  • Practicing negative visualization (5 minutes daily)
  • Journaling about control vs. non-control

Track your experience and insights each day.

Exercise 2: Mindfulness Bell

Set a random timer 3 times throughout your day. When it rings:

  1. Stop and take three conscious breaths
  2. Notice your current physical sensations
  3. Observe your current emotional state
  4. Note what thoughts are present
  5. Return to your activity with renewed awareness

Exercise 3: Philosophical Dialogue

Have a conversation with yourself from three perspectives:

  • The Stoic: Focus on acceptance and virtue
  • The Buddhist: Emphasize compassion and non-attachment
  • The Existentialist: Highlight freedom and responsibility

What insights emerge from each perspective?
